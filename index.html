Homotopy

(A squiggle is the same as another squiggle)

A homotopy is a continuous map between two spaces. These spaces are just topological spaces, like the two on the right.
Formally, a homotopy is a continuous function between two topological spaces $X$ and $Y$ parameterised by some time value, $0 \le t \le 1$. $$ H : X \times [0, 1] \to Y $$
We now ask, under what metric are two things similar? Well, we call spaces similar if they have the same fundemental group.
Let us take some closed paths over the objects, that is we run around and take any path where we start and end at the same point. Then the fundemental group is the collection of all these different paths, up to homotopy. This has some sort of structure. This is what we call a group structure in maths.
A group has an idea of addition. To add paths we just line them up end to end,
Further, a group have the following structure,
  • Closure - Adding two paths makes another path.
  • Inverse - to undo a path, run in the opposing direction
  • Identity - do nothing. This like adding zero.
  • A group also has associativity. That is, if you have three paths $p_1$, $p_2$ and $p_3$ then if you run $p_1$ then $p_2$ then $p_3$ that is the same as running $p_2$ then $p$
The two paths of a circle are just running around in a forward direction and running around in a negative direction or doing nothing. Hence, we have the path $1$, forward, $-1$, backwards and $0$, do nothing. This forms a group. The fundemental group. This group is exactly the integers, $\mathbb{\Z} = \{0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \dots\}$. Hence $\pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$.

The Siefert-Van Kampen Theorem

We now introduce something that is a keystone idea in Algebraic Topology. The Siefert-Van Kampen Theorem.
  • Wedge Sum
  • Siefert-Van Kampen
The wedge sum is defined as follows. Take two objects, then the wedge sum of these two objects is just the two objects touching at a point. If $X$ and $Y$ are spaces, the wedge sum is notated as, $$ X \vee Y $$
The Siefert-Van Kampen Theorem states that if we have space that is just a wedge sum, then the fundemental group is just some product of the fundemental groups of both spaces, $$ \pi_1(X \vee Y) = \pi_1(X) * \pi_2(Y) $$
So, the fundemental group of a figure eight is, $$ \pi_1(S^1 \vee S^1) = \pi_1(S^1) * \pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z} $$
The fundemental group of a figure eight has four elements: $0$, do nothing, $A$ go around the left lobe, $B$ go around the right lobe and $X$ go around the whole figure. This is the free group on two characters, we denote this as, $$ \langle A, B \rangle. $$
We call the figure of eight, the 2-rose (a rose with two petals). If we consider roses with more petals, which parkruns usually are, we are going to have, $$ \begin{align*} \pi_1 (S^1 \vee S^1 \vee \dots \vee S^1) &= \pi_1(S^1) * \pi_1(S^1) * \dots * \pi_1(S^1) \\ &= \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z} * \dots * \mathbb{Z} \end{align*}. $$ This then becomes the free group on $n$ letters.

ASIDE: COVERING SPACES

Here's a thing. You usually go around a track more than once on Parkrun, does this affect things? The answer is, it doesn't. You may ask me why? That's because of something we call covering spaces.
Take your parkrun course and look at it as a slinky. When you pull it upwards you see something very similar to the image on the last page. Now cut the slinky at the start of the course (if it overlaps) and then you will have a few copies of one lap of the course. We are interested in the shape of a lap and so it doesn't matter!

THERE ARE ONLY $N$ PARKRUN COURSES!

We are going to consider some parkrun courses up to homotopy. Here are some of the different parkrun courses,
The non-closed loop. Here we have a loop that contracts just back to a point. An example of this is my favourite parkrun, Penrose Estate.
A non-overlapping loop. This is *just* a circle. An example of this is Gunnersbury Parkrun,
One crossing loop. This is a figure 8. An example of this is Heartlands Parkrun, my home parkrun.
